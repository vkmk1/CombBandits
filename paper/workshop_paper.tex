\begin{abstract}
Combinatorial semi-bandits over $d$ base arms with superarms of size $m$ suffer a curse of dimensionality: standard CUCB achieves $\tilde{O}(\sqrt{md \cdot T})$ regret while the exponential action space makes enumeration intractable.
Large language models can generate combinatorially structured suggestions reflecting world-knowledge priors, yet these suggestions are systematically unreliable.
We introduce \emph{action-space corruption}---qualitatively different from reward corruption---where an external oracle corrupts the \emph{action proposal} rather than the \emph{reward observation}.
We define a graded quality model for the Combinatorial LLM Oracle (CLO) and propose \textbf{LLM-CUCB-AT}, which adaptively trusts or hedges against the oracle using a composite trust score combining inter-query consistency with posterior validation.
We prove: (i) dimension reduction from $d$ to the oracle's effective support when reliable; (ii) graceful degradation to vanilla CUCB under adversarial corruption; (iii) a matching $\Omega(\varepsilon m / \Delta_{\min})$ lower bound for action corruption; and (iv) an $O(\sqrt{T})$-query schedule reducing LLM cost.
Experiments on synthetic semi-bandits, news recommendation, and influence maximization validate the theoretical predictions.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%=============================================================================

Combinatorial semi-bandits~\citep{chen2013combinatorial,kveton2015tight} model sequential decision problems where a learner selects a subset of $m$ arms from $d$ candidates, observes per-arm rewards, and minimizes cumulative regret against the best fixed subset.
Applications include online recommendation (select $m$ items), influence maximization (select $m$ seed nodes), and resource allocation.
The minimax regret scales as $\tilde{O}(\sqrt{mdT})$, and the combinatorial explosion $\binom{d}{m}$ makes full enumeration intractable.

LLMs, when prompted with problem structure, produce combinatorially structured suggestions reflecting world knowledge unavailable to the bandit algorithm.
Yet these suggestions are systematically unreliable: LLMs hallucinate, exhibit recency bias, and produce inconsistent outputs.
Worse, an LLM can be \emph{consistently wrong}---deterministically returning the same suboptimal set---a failure mode that simple consistency checks miss entirely.

\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We formalize \emph{action-space corruption} in combinatorial bandits---qualitatively different from the reward corruption of~\citet{lykouris2018}---and define a graded quality model for the Combinatorial LLM Oracle (CLO) parameterized by overlap fraction, corruption rate, and consistency (Section~\ref{sec:model}).

    \item We propose \textbf{LLM-CUCB-AT}, which uses a composite trust score $\tau_t = \min(\kappa_t, \rho_t)$ combining consistency (does the LLM agree with itself?) and posterior validation (does the LLM agree with evidence?) to adaptively hedge against unreliable oracles (Section~\ref{sec:algorithm}).

    \item We prove tight regret bounds showing dimension reduction under reliability, graceful degradation under corruption, and a matching information-theoretic lower bound establishing that the corruption cost $\Omega(\varepsilon m / \Delta_{\min})$ is unavoidable (Section~\ref{sec:theory}).

    \item Experiments on synthetic semi-bandits, news recommendation, and influence maximization validate the theoretical predictions (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Problem Setting and Oracle Model}
\label{sec:model}
%=============================================================================

\paragraph{Stochastic combinatorial semi-bandit.}
Ground set $[d]$, superarm size $m$, horizon $T$.
At round $t$, the learner selects $S_t \in \Fset$ with $|S_t| = m$, observes rewards $X_{i,t} \sim \nu_i$ (mean $\mu_i \in [0,1]$) for $i \in S_t$.
Optimal superarm: $\Sstar = \arg\max_{S \in \Fset} \sum_{i \in S} \mu_i$.
Regret: $R_T = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \sum_{i \in \Sstar} \mu_i - \sum_{i \in S_t} \mu_i$.

\begin{definition}[Combinatorial LLM Oracle]
A CLO maps context $C_t$ to a distribution over feasible sets.
Querying produces a candidate set $\SL \in \Fset$.
The \emph{overlap fraction} $q_t = |\SL \cap \Sstar|/m \in [0,1]$ measures partial reliability.
The CLO has corruption rate $\varepsilon = \Prob(q_t = 0)$ and mean quality $\bar{q} = \E[q_t]$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Composite Trust Score]
Given $K$ re-queries producing sets $\SL_{(1)}, \ldots, \SL_{(K)}$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Consistency: $\kappa_t = |\bigcap_{k=1}^{K} \SL_{(k)}| / m$.
    \item Posterior validation: $\rho_t = \sum_{i \in \SL} \hat{\mu}_{i,t} \big/ \max_{S \in \Fset} \sum_{i \in S} \hat{\mu}_{i,t}$.
    \item Composite trust: $\tau_t = \min(\kappa_t, \rho_t)$.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\textbf{Why both scores are necessary.}
Consistency alone fails against a \emph{deterministically wrong} oracle ($\kappa_t = 1$ but the set is wrong).
Posterior validation alone is unreliable early when empirical estimates are noisy.
The composite score is conservative: trust the oracle only when it agrees with itself \emph{and} with accumulated evidence.

\paragraph{Action corruption vs.\ reward corruption.}
The corrupted bandit literature~\citep{lykouris2018,he2022nearly} studies adversarial corruption of \emph{observed rewards}, with additive cost $O(C)$.
We study corruption of the \emph{action proposal}---the set of arms the learner considers.
The learner retains uncorrupted reward observations.
Our lower bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:lower_bound}) shows the cost structure is multiplicative in $T$, not additive.

\section{Algorithm: LLM-CUCB-AT}
\label{sec:algorithm}
%=============================================================================

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{LLM-CUCB-AT: Combinatorial UCB with Adaptive LLM Trust}
\label{alg:llm_cucb_at}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Ground set $[d]$, superarm size $m$, horizon $T$, CLO $\Lset$, re-queries $K$, max hedge $h_{\max}$, init rounds $T_0$
\STATE \textbf{Phase 1: Initialization} (rounds $1, \ldots, T_0$): Round-robin to sample all arms
\STATE \textbf{Phase 2: Adaptive Play} (rounds $T_0+1, \ldots, T$):
\FOR{$t = T_0 + 1, \ldots, T$}
    \STATE Query $\Lset(C_t)$ $K$ times $\to$ sets $\SL_{(1)}, \ldots, \SL_{(K)}$; set $\SL \gets \SL_{(1)}$
    \STATE $\kappa_t \gets |\bigcap_{k} \SL_{(k)}| / m$; \quad $\rho_t \gets \sum_{i \in \SL} \hat{\mu}_{i,t} \big/ \max_{S} \sum_{i \in S} \hat{\mu}_{i,t}$
    \STATE $\tau_t \gets \min(\kappa_t, \rho_t)$; \quad $h_t \gets \lceil h_{\max} \cdot (1 - \tau_t) \rceil$
    \STATE $\Hucb \gets$ top-$h_t$ arms in $[d] \setminus \SL$ by UCB index
    \STATE $\Dt \gets \SL \cup \Hucb$
    \STATE $S_t \gets$ top-$m$ arms in $\Dt$ by UCB index $\hat{\mu}_{i,t} + \sqrt{2\log(t) / N_{i,t}}$
    \STATE Observe rewards; update $\hat{\mu}_{i,t}$, $N_{i,t}$
    \STATE \textbf{Corruption detection:} if windowed regret $> \gamma_t$, next round fallback to CUCB on $[d]$
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\textbf{Design rationale.}
The hedge size $h_t = \lceil h_{\max}(1 - \tau_t) \rceil$ continuously interpolates between full trust ($h_t = 0$, effective dimension $m$) and full exploration ($h_t = h_{\max} = \lceil\sqrt{d}\rceil$).
The UCB-ranked hedge ensures high-confidence arms from data are always considered.
The corruption detector uses a sliding window of empirical regret with threshold $\gamma_t = \sqrt{m\log(t)/W}$, calibrated for $O(1/t^2)$ false alarm rate.

\section{Theoretical Results}
\label{sec:theory}
%=============================================================================

\begin{theorem}[Minimax Regret Bound]
\label{thm:main}
Under an $\varepsilon$-corrupted CLO with mean quality $\bar{q}$, LLM-CUCB-AT achieves:
\[
\E[R_T] \leq \tilde{O}\!\left(\sqrt{m(m + h_{\max}(1-\bar{q})) \cdot T_{\mathrm{explore}}}\right) + O\!\left(\frac{\varepsilon m T}{\Delta_{\min}}\right) + O(d \log d),
\]
where $T_{\mathrm{explore}} = |\{t > T_0 : h_t > 0\}|$ and $h_{\max} = \lceil\sqrt{d}\rceil$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{corollary}[Two-Regime Behavior]
\label{cor:two_regime}
(i) \textbf{Perfect oracle} ($\varepsilon = 0$, $\bar{q} = 1$): $\E[R_T] = O(d\log d)$ (initialization cost only).
(ii) \textbf{Reliable oracle} ($\varepsilon < 0.2$, $\bar{q} > 0.7$): regret is $\tilde{O}(\sqrt{m(m + 0.2\sqrt{d})T})$, significantly below $\tilde{O}(\sqrt{mdT})$.
(iii) \textbf{Adversarial oracle} ($\varepsilon > 0.5$): degrades gracefully to $\tilde{O}(\sqrt{mdT})$ via fallback.
(iv) \textbf{Consistently wrong oracle} ($\kappa=1$, $\varepsilon=1$): posterior validation detects the mismatch; the algorithm does not suffer linear regret.
\end{corollary}

\begin{theorem}[Lower Bound for Action Corruption]
\label{thm:lower_bound}
For any algorithm using an $\varepsilon$-corrupted CLO, there exists an instance with gap $\Delta_{\min}$ such that:
$\E[R_T] \geq \Omega(\varepsilon m \log T / \Delta_{\min})$.
No algorithm achieves $o(\varepsilon m T / \Delta_{\min})$ uniformly without knowing $\varepsilon$.
\end{theorem}

The lower bound is proved via a two-instance construction where the CLO behaves identically but optimal arms differ, requiring $\Omega(1/\Delta^2)$ samples to distinguish.
This establishes that the corruption cost in Theorem~\ref{thm:main} is tight.

\begin{theorem}[Query Schedule]
\label{thm:query_schedule}
With inter-query gaps $\Dquery_j = \lceil c\sqrt{j}\rceil$, the total CLO queries are $O(\sqrt{T})$ with an additive $O(m\sqrt{T})$ regret increase.
\end{theorem}

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
%=============================================================================

We evaluate LLM-CUCB-AT on synthetic semi-bandits with controlled corruption, simulated news recommendation, and simulated influence maximization.
All experiments use a simulated CLO (parameterized noisy oracle) to validate theoretical predictions under controlled conditions.
All results report mean $\pm$ standard error over 20--30 seeds.

\subsection{Oracle Safety Under Corruption (exp7)}

\paragraph{Setup.}
Bernoulli arms with $d=100$, $m=10$, $T=30{,}000$, 30 seeds.
Simulated CLO under four corruption models: clean ($\varepsilon=0$), uniform ($\varepsilon=0.2$), adversarial ($\varepsilon=0.3$), and consistently wrong ($\varepsilon=1$, $\kappa=1$).

Under the consistently wrong oracle (top center), LLM-Greedy suffers linear regret ($\sim$70K) while LLM-CUCB-AT detects the bad oracle and hedges ($\sim$23K).}

\paragraph{Results.}
Table~\ref{tab:main_results} and Figure~\ref{fig:regret_curves} show LLM-CUCB-AT achieves 3--5$\times$ lower regret than oracle-following baselines (LLM-Greedy, ELLM-Adapted) across all corruption types.
The composite trust mechanism prevents catastrophic oracle-following: under the consistently wrong oracle, LLM-Greedy incurs $\sim$70K regret (linear) while LLM-CUCB-AT hedges to $\sim$23K.
All pairwise comparisons between LLM-CUCB-AT and oracle-following methods are statistically significant (Wilcoxon signed-rank, $p < 10^{-9}$, Cohen's $d = 4$--$7$).

CUCB ($\sim$7K) outperforms LLM-CUCB-AT ($\sim$14--24K) with the simulated oracle because the simulated CLO is a parameterized coin flip---it adds corruption noise without providing useful world knowledge.
This motivates the need for real LLM experiments where the oracle has genuine domain reasoning (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).
Warm-Start CTS dominates because the simulated oracle reveals $\Sstar$ in a single query; this advantage is specific to simulation and would not hold with a real LLM.

Figure~\ref{fig:trust} shows the composite trust score dynamics.
Under the consistently wrong oracle, the consistency score $\kappa_t \approx 1$ throughout---a pure consistency check would incorrectly trust this oracle.
However, posterior validation $\rho_t$ drops as empirical estimates concentrate, correctly triggering a hedge.
This validates the key design insight: both components are necessary.

\subsection{Simulated News Recommendation and Influence Maximization}

\paragraph{News recommendation.}
Simulated MIND-like environment: $d=200$ candidate articles with category structure and user preferences, $m=5$ displayed per session, $T=2{,}000$, 20 seeds.
Under partial overlap corruption ($\varepsilon = 0.15$), LLM-CUCB-AT achieves regret of $1{,}931 \pm 82$, comparable to CUCB ($1{,}920 \pm 80$) and 16\% lower than LLM-Greedy ($2{,}296 \pm 142$).
Thompson Sampling methods (CTS: $1{,}207$; Warm-Start CTS: $1{,}144$) outperform UCB-based methods on this structured task.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/regret_multipanel_mind.pdf}
    \caption{Cumulative regret on simulated news recommendation ($d=200$, $m=5$, $T=2{,}000$). LLM-CUCB-AT (red) is comparable to CUCB (blue) and consistently outperforms LLM-Greedy (purple) and ELLM-Adapted (teal). Thompson Sampling methods (CTS, Warm-Start CTS) benefit most from the structured category-user preference reward model.}
    \label{fig:mind}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Influence maximization.}
Simulated social network with planted community structure: $d=200$ candidate seed nodes, $m=10$, $T=5{,}000$, 20 seeds.
Under partial overlap corruption ($\varepsilon = 0.2$), LLM-CUCB-AT ($5{,}203 \pm 97$) matches CUCB ($5{,}142 \pm 91$) and achieves 37\% lower regret than LLM-Greedy ($8{,}256 \pm 319$).

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/regret_multipanel_infmax.pdf}
    \caption{Cumulative regret on simulated influence maximization ($d=200$, $m=10$, $T=5{,}000$). The structured reward with clear community clusters favors Thompson Sampling. LLM-CUCB-AT (red) matches CUCB (blue) and achieves 37\% lower regret than oracle-following methods (LLM-Greedy, ELLM-Adapted) which suffer from blindly following the simulated oracle's corrupt suggestions.}
    \label{fig:infmax}
\end{figure}

In both domains, LLM-CUCB-AT provides a safe middle ground: it never performs catastrophically (unlike LLM-Greedy and ELLM-Adapted, which incur 60--100\% higher regret) while maintaining comparable performance to CUCB.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%=============================================================================

\paragraph{Combinatorial semi-bandits.}
CUCB~\citep{chen2013combinatorial} achieves $\tilde{O}(\sqrt{mdT})$ regret; \citet{kveton2015tight} prove matching lower bounds.
Our contribution is orthogonal: external structured priors reduce the effective dimension.

\paragraph{Corrupted bandits.}
\citet{lykouris2018} study \emph{reward} corruption (additive cost $O(C)$); \citet{he2022nearly} extend to near-optimal rates.
We study \emph{action} corruption with multiplicative cost structure (Theorem~\ref{thm:lower_bound}).

\paragraph{LLMs for optimization.}
FunSearch~\citep{romera2024mathematical} and OPRO~\citep{yang2024large} use LLMs in offline or one-shot settings without sequential regret.
ELLM~\citep{du2023guiding} uses LLMs for exploration in MDPs without regret bounds.
We operate in an online loop with formal guarantees and a corruption model.

\paragraph{Bandits with expert advice.}
EXP4~\citep{auer2002nonstochastic} handles adversarial experts at $O(\sqrt{T\log K})$ rates.
Our stochastic setting with a single unreliable combinatorial oracle enables tighter bounds.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%=============================================================================

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Our theory assumes fixed $\Sstar$ (stochastic setting) and stationary oracle quality.
The $\alpha$-approximation oracle assumption is standard but restrictive for some combinatorial structures.

\paragraph{When to use LLM-CUCB-AT.}
The algorithm benefits when: (i) $d$ is large enough that CUCB converges slowly; (ii) the LLM has domain-relevant knowledge; (iii) the characterization $\varepsilon < \Delta_{\min}\sqrt{d/m}$ holds (testable precondition).

\paragraph{Broader impact.}
Our framework provides formal guarantees for a common informal practice---using LLMs to guide combinatorial decisions---and makes reliability requirements explicit.
The corruption model applies beyond LLMs to any unreliable oracle (e.g., human experts with known error rates).
